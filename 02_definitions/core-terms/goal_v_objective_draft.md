Yes — they are close enough that you do not need both unless they are doing different work.

The cleanest distinction is:

**Goal** = the end being aimed at.
**Objective** = the defined, often more operational form of that end.

So in your framework:

A **goal** is broader, organizing, and directional. It tells you what the system is trying to move toward.

An **objective** is narrower, more specified, and more legible as a concrete target or success condition. It tells you what must be accomplished, demonstrated, or satisfied in order to serve the goal.

So I would phrase it like this:

**Goal** is the target state, outcome, or condition toward which choice, effort, and coordination are directed.

**Objective** is a defined target or success condition set in service of a goal, narrow enough to guide evaluation or action more concretely.

Another clean way to feel the difference:

A goal answers: **what are we trying to reach?**
An objective answers: **what specifically must happen to count as progress toward it?**

Example in AI Foundations:

Goal: preserve origin-shaped coherence.
Objective: maintain the same definitions, boundaries, and continuity markers across equivalent conditions.

So the goal is the higher-order end. The objective is the more operational target under it.

My honest instinct, though, is that **goal** matters more than **objective** in your book unless you are going to define evaluation, protocol design, or measurable tests. **Objective** starts sounding a little more managerial. **Goal** sounds more structural and alive.
